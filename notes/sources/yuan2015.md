# Yuan et al. 2015 : Local–overall interactive buckling behaviour of welded stainless steel I-section columns

## Metadata

* **Date read:** 17/06/2026

## 1. Summary: what was tested

This study investigated the local–overall interactive buckling behaviour of welded stainless steel I-section columns. The experimental programme tested 10 pin-ended long columns fabricated from hot-rolled plates, covering two grades: austenitic EN 1.4301 and duplex EN 1.4462. The members were designed with varying lengths and cross-sectional slendernesses to intentionally induce interactive local and global buckling about the minor axis. The study also included a massive parametric Finite Element Analysis (FEA) comprising 342 models to assess current design codes.

## 2. Material properties

* **Experimental:** Derived from flat tensile coupons cut directly from the hot-rolled plates used to fabricate the sections. The authors tested both longitudinal (LT) and transverse (TT) directions, revealing moderate anisotropy.
* **Reporting:** Full primary ($E_0$, $\sigma_{0.2}$, $n$) and secondary ($\sigma_{1.0}$, $\sigma_u$) properties are reported per direction and grade in Table 1.
* **Selection:** Since columns are axially loaded along their longitudinal axis, the Longitudinal Tension (LT) properties must be used for data extraction. (Stub column compressive properties were not reported in this paper, though they reference a separate study).

## 3. Ramberg-Osgood exponent n (provenance flag)

n_source = measured

* **Reasoning:** The authors state they used the two-stage modified Ramberg-Osgood model "to represent the measured stress-strain curves," and they explicitly report $n$ values (e.g., 5.8 and 7.4) in Table 1. 

## 4. Batch structure and study_id groupings

There are 2 distinct physical material batches corresponding to the two hot-rolled steel grades, each sharing a unique set of LT coupon results.

* `yuan2015_A`: Austenitic 1.4301 welded I-sections
* `yuan2015_B`: Duplex 1.4462 welded I-sections

## 5. Geometry and test setup

* **Geometry:** Measured dimensions ($b_f$, $h$, $t_f$, $t_w$) are fully tabulated for all specimens in Table 2. Flange and web thicknesses were uniformly 6.00 mm. Following the data dictionary for I-sections, flange width $b_f$ maps to $b$, overall depth $h$ maps to $h$, and the thicknesses map to $t_f$ and $t_w$ (with $t$ also populated as 6.00).
* **Boundary conditions:** Pin-ended (pin-pin). The columns allowed free rotation about the minor axis. 20 mm thick carbon steel end plates were welded to the ends for bearing.
* **Effective length ($L_e$):** Reported directly by the authors in Table 2.

## 6. Initial geometric imperfections

* **Reporting:** The authors rigorously measured and tabulated imperfection metrics in Table 4. However, their notation differs from the dataset dictionary and requires careful mapping:
* The authors' $v_0$ is the global bow/curvature amplitude. This maps to the dataset's $w_0$.
* The authors' $w_0$ is the local imperfection amplitude. This is ignored for our global-focused dataset.
* The authors' $e_c$ is the applied functional load eccentricity. This maps to the dataset's $w_e$.
* The authors' $e_{Eq}$ is the explicitly calculated equivalent total initial eccentricity ($e_c + v_0$). This maps directly to the dataset's $w_{total}$.



## 7. Failure modes and exclusions

* `n_specimens` (total in paper): 352 (10 physical tests + 342 FEA models).
* `n_included` (after filtering): 10
* **Exclusions and reasons:** all 342 numerical FEA models are strictly excluded to prevent artificial dataset inflation and data leakage. The 10 physical tests all failed by combined local and overall flexural buckling. They will be included in the raw extraction and explicitly labeled as `interactive` in the `failure_mode` column.

## 8. FEA details

* **Validation:** Highly accurate. The mean FE-to-test ultimate load ratio was 0.98.
* **Models:** ABAQUS using S4R shell elements.
* **Crucial Residual Stress Finding:** The authors found that FE models without residual stresses overestimated interactive buckling resistance by an average of 18%. This highlights a major difference between welded sections (where tensile/compressive residual stresses severely trigger early local buckling) and cold-formed sections.

## 9. Data extraction notes

* **Tables:** Material properties (Table 1, LT rows). Geometries (Table 2). Imperfections and eccentricities (Table 4). Ultimate experimental loads $N_{u,Exp}$ (Table 6).
* **Units:** Modulus $E_0$ is reported in MPa. Stresses in MPa. Loads in kN. Dimensions in mm. No conversions required.
* **Buckling Axis:** All columns buckled about the minor axis, so `buckling_axis` must be populated as `minor`.

## 10. Judgement calls and flags

* **Material Selection Constraint:** Extracted primary material properties from the Longitudinal Tension (LT) flat coupons (Table 1).
* **Imperfection Mapping:** Carefully mapped the paper's global imperfection $v_0$ to $w_0$, $e_c$ to $w_e$, and $e_{Eq}$ to $w_{total}$. The paper's $w_0$ (local imperfection) was intentionally skipped.
* **FEA Exclusion:** Excluded the 342 parametric FEA models.
* **Failure Mode Label:** Assigned `interactive` to all 10 extracted rows, as the paper's explicit aim and observation was local-overall interactive buckling.

## 11. General notes and useful for later

* **Welded vs. Cold-Formed Behaviour:** The paper notes that the compressive residual stresses near the flange tips and towards the centre of the web in welded sections strongly promote early local buckling, causing a rapid loss of effective cross-section area. This is why interactive buckling is so severe here compared to equivalent cold-formed sections.
* **Code Performance:** The current EN 1993-1-4 provisions were found to be overly conservative for interactive buckling. The authors proposed new separate design curves for austenitic ($\alpha = 0.76$, $\lambda_0 = 0.35$) and duplex ($\alpha = 0.49$, $\lambda_0 = 0.35$) grades. Your ML models may organically capture this grade-based divergence without needing separate hardcoded curves.
